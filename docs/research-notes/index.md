# Research Notes

This section is for essays, research-driven commentary, and analytical notes that sit adjacent to the case studies in the portfolio.

## What belongs here

Use **Research Notes** for writing that is not primarily a product-doc sample, such as:

- analysis of technical writing or documentation research
- essays on AI, developer documentation, or knowledge-work workflows
- commentary that synthesizes a paper, standard, or tooling trend for practitioners
- longer-form pieces that show analytical depth rather than product-documentation execution

Use **Case Studies** for deliverables such as guides, redlines, conceptual documentation, and documentation exercises tied to specific products or tools.

## Suggested publishing categories

You can keep everything in this folder, but it helps to think in three content modes:

- **Research Notes** — synthesis of papers, tools, or technical trends
- **Essays** — opinionated but evidence-driven pieces with a stronger argument arc
- **Working Notes** — shorter exploratory drafts that are still polished enough to publish

## Recommended structure for each entry

Aim for a consistent structure so the section feels curated rather than like a loose blog.

1. **Title** — specific and descriptive
2. **Short setup** — 1–3 sentences explaining the problem or question
3. **Why it matters** — the practical stakes for the intended audience
4. **Main analysis** — your synthesis, argument, or framework
5. **Implications** — what should the reader do differently?
6. **Source / references** — especially if the piece is research-based

## Workflow suggestion

When adapting material from LinkedIn or other short-form posts:

- start from the strongest core claim
- remove platform-specific framing
- tighten the argument arc around one main thesis
- add source links or references directly in the piece
- make the artifact stand on its own without requiring social context

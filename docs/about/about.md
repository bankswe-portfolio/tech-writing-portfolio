# Workflow & About

This portfolio is designed to show both the writing itself and the workflow I use to produce and ship documentation.

## What the portfolio is for

The case studies on this site are not random writing samples. Each one is here to demonstrate a specific documentation capability:

- explaining a complex concept clearly
- building practical, task-oriented guidance
- editing and restructuring existing material to improve usability
- presenting technical information with enough rigor for real product contexts

## How the samples were created

The samples in this portfolio come from a mix of independent projects and application-oriented exercises. They are intended to show how I think through documentation problems, not just how I write sentences.

Across the portfolio, the recurring questions are:

- Who is the user?
- What are they trying to do?
- What background can I assume?
- What structure will reduce friction fastest?
- What level of detail is necessary for accuracy without overwhelming the reader?

## Docs-as-code workflow

This site runs on **MkDocs** with the **Material for MkDocs** theme and is deployed through **GitHub Pages**.

Content is written in Markdown, tracked in Git, and published from the repository. That reflects how I prefer to work on product teams:

- documentation versioned alongside source and reviews
- changes tracked in commits and pull requests
- lightweight publishing workflows that are easy to maintain
- documentation treated as a product asset rather than a static afterthought

## Editorial approach

My documentation work tends to prioritize four things:

- **clarity:** remove ambiguity and reduce reader effort
- **usability:** make the next step obvious
- **technical credibility:** preserve accuracy and respect domain complexity
- **context:** explain not only what to do, but when and why it matters

## Background

I’m a technical writer and former research engineer with experience across developer SDKs, APIs, avionics standards, biomedical platforms, and defense-adjacent systems. That combination of writing practice and engineering depth shapes the way I approach documentation: understand the system first, then make it understandable to someone else.

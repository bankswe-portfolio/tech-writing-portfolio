Framework Essays — Concept Work

Governing
LLM-Assisted Work

Most advice about using LLMs in knowledge work focuses on better prompts. These essays focus on what better prompts cannot fix.

When an LLM is doing iterative intellectual labor on a long-lived project — drafting, revising, auditing, proposing alternatives — a specific failure regime emerges that is invisible from inside a single conversation. Documents get cleaner. Scope gets tighter. Everything reads as progress. But the same substantive defects persist in relabeled form, the project quietly becomes a different project, and there is no structural mechanism to detect any of it.

This is a governance problem, not a prompting problem. These three essays describe the framework, patterns, and architecture developed to address it — derived from direct application on a live research program, not from theoretical preferences.

The Frozen Anchor: A Document Design Pattern for Long-Lived AI-Assisted Projects

A project's biggest risk is not that it fails — it is that it quietly becomes a different project. This essay introduces the Frozen Anchor: a four-section document design pattern that records what a project agreed to be before revision pressure can distort it. Covers the pattern's anatomy, the distinction between acceptable narrowing and drift, version discipline, and a three-question diagnostic test for when the project feels unrecognizable.

A Control Framework for LLM-Assisted Research: Governing Research Definition Under Weak Supervision

LLMs are unreliable in a way that is recursively dangerous: they produce confident, coherent, load-bearing falsehoods that accumulate silently across revision cycles. This essay builds a control-theory model of the research governance problem — mapping reference signals, sensors, state estimation, bounded correction, and anti-windup protections onto a working governance architecture with twelve requirements, three failure modes to guard against, and five explicit decision states for when revision should stop.

Designing Multi-Role AI Collaboration Workflows: A Four-Layer Architecture for Solo Research Programs

The default AI collaboration posture — one context, one conversation, handling everything — produces closed-loop validation at the workflow level. This essay describes a four-role architecture developed on a live project: a research layer, a build layer, an ideation layer, and a separate authorization gate that is the only context permitted to commit outputs to the project record. Each role is defined by what it cannot do as much as what it can.

About this work

What these are

Concept essays and framework notes developed through direct application on a live, solo LLM-assisted research program. The frameworks, patterns, and protocols described here were built because they were needed — each rule documents a failure mode that existed before the rule did.

What these are not

These are not validated research papers. The frameworks have been applied and refined on one project; they have not been tested across multiple programs, benchmarked against alternatives, or subjected to independent empirical evaluation. They are offered as concept work and qualified practitioner reflection.

The provenance

All three essays are derived from working artifacts: a live research protocol document, a frozen identity anchor maintained across multiple audit cycles, and an implementation log that records both decisions made and alternatives rejected. The source material predates the essays.

Where this fits

The adjacent literatures are LLM auditing, research proposal validation, qualitative convergence theory, and weak supervision frameworks. This work sits between them rather than inside any one of them — the combination of frozen identity anchor, persistent defect register, and cross-cycle convergence judgment does not appear to exist as a packaged framework elsewhere.
